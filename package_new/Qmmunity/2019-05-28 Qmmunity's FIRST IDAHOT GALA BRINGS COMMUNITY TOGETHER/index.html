Qmmunity's FIRST IDAHOT GALA BRINGS COMMUNITY TOGETHER

毕雯珊Sav Qmmunity
2019年05月28日 12:43



Dazzling in gowns and suits, corsets and cloaks, Shanghai’s LGBTQ and ally community gathered together for China's first IDAHOT gala at the Renaissance Shanghai Yu Garden Hotel to celebrate May 17, the International Day Against Homophobia, Transphobia and Biphobia (IDAHOT), as well as our  Qmmunity five-year anniversary.


Aside from raising support for Qmmunity and LGBTQ in China, the gala represented a safe space for members of marginalized communities to share their stories and identities without fear or shame.

 

“This event is a true testimony to what happens when the LGBTQ community and allies come together to celebrate identity,” event organizer Gabriel said. “It combined so many elements of the community and created a platform for both allies and Chinese LGBTQ to be heard.” 


IDAHOT GALA


The Gala opened with a moving performance from renowned singer Denise Mininfield. Notable speakers included the US Consul General Sean Stein and Canadian Consul Jose Si. Si presented Qmmunity, which Gabriel accepted, with a unique commemorative Canadian coin representing lesbian, gay, transgender, queer and two-spirited people. Chinese PFLAG (Parents and Friends of Lesbians and Gays) volunteer Lao Duan spoke of his LGBTQ advocacy efforts in China, while queer activist Taolegeriler, currently based in Guangzhou, spoke of their grassroots organization efforts in western Chinese provinces.

 

US Consul General Sean Stein has long been a vocal ally for LGBTQ communities. Speaking in both Chinese and English, Stein’s speech at the gala was greeted by applause, laughter, and even friendly boos when he opened up to the audience about his initial hesitancy to engage queer communities. “If I can change, so can other people out there,” Stein encouraged.

On May 17, 1990, the World Health Organization stopped classifying homosexuality as a disease, inspiring later queer activists to celebrate May 17 as IDAHOT. In China, homosexuality was decriminalized in 1997, but challenges still endure for queer Chinese. 


IDAHOT GALA


“China often gets a bad reputation for LGBTQ,” explained Gabriel. “But this event helped prove that actually there is room for celebration on a massive level and the predominantly Chinese audience shoes how China is becoming a more accepting and welcoming place where people feel they can truly be themselves in such a public way.”

 

Expat attendees and straight allies also lent their support to the community. “It’s really great to find a gay-friendly environment in China and donate to such a great cause,” one attendee Jennifer Long said. “I brought my friends here where they could be completely open and hear other people’s true stories. I really want to let them feel comfortable.”


After the IDAHOT Gala, Qmmunity plans to capitalize on the moment and excitement the event generated to continue to pursue more community connections in China and continue to develop the Qmmunity app to connect queer communities across the world.
